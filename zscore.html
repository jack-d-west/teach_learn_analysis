Z-Score

The z-score, also known as the standard score, measures how far a data point is from the mean in terms of standard deviations. It tells us if a data point is typical or unusual within a data set.

Formula for Z-score:

Z-score = (X - μ) / σ

Where:

  • X is the value in question,
  • μ is the mean of the dataset,
  • σ is the standard deviation.

Why is the Z-Score Useful?

With a z-score, you can quickly compare different data points, even from different data sets, to understand relative performance. For example, in test grading, a z-score of +1.5 tells the student and teacher that the score is well above average, indicating strong performance without relying solely on raw scores.

Z-Score Examples

Example 1: Heights in a Population

Scenario: A survey measures heights in a population, with a mean height of 170 cm and a standard deviation of 8 cm. If Lisa’s height is 182 cm, is she taller than average?

Calculate the Z-score:

Z = (182 - 170) / 8 = 12 / 8 = 1.5

Interpretation: Lisa’s height of 182 cm is 1.5 standard deviations above the mean, placing her taller than approximately 93% of people in the population.

Example 2: Customer Satisfaction Score

Scenario: A company surveys customer satisfaction, scoring it from 1 to 100. The average score is 75, with a standard deviation of 5. If a particular branch scores 85, how exceptional is this score?

Calculate the Z-score:

Z = (85 - 75) / 5 = 10 / 5 = 2.0

Interpretation: A z-score of 2.0 means this branch’s score is 2 standard deviations above the mean, placing it in the top 97.7% of scores, indicating exceptional customer satisfaction.

Example 3: Comparing Two Exam Scores

Scenario: Sara took two different exams: math and English. Her math class has a mean of 75 and a standard deviation of 10, while the English class has a mean of 60 with a standard deviation of 8. Sara scored 85 in math and 68 in English. Which exam did she perform better on relative to her classmates?

Calculate Z-scores for each exam:

  • Math: Z = (85 - 75) / 10 = 1.0
  • English: Z = (68 - 60) / 8 = 1.0

Interpretation: In both cases, her z-score is 1.0, meaning she performed 1 standard deviation above the mean in each subject. This shows she did equally well relative to her peers in both subjects.

Example 4: Body Temperature

Scenario: The average human body temperature is 98.6°F with a standard deviation of 0.6°F. After a marathon, a runner’s temperature is measured at 100.2°F. Should this temperature be a concern?

Calculate the Z-score:

Z = (100.2 - 98.6) / 0.6 ≈ 2.67

Interpretation: A z-score of 2.67 indicates that the runner’s temperature is higher than 99% of typical body temperatures, potentially suggesting a medical concern like heat exhaustion or dehydration.

Example 5: Investment Returns

Scenario: A mutual fund’s average annual return over the past five years is 7%, with a standard deviation of 3%. If the fund returns 12% this year, how does this year’s performance compare?

Calculate the Z-score:

Z = (12 - 7) / 3 ≈ 1.67

Interpretation: A z-score of 1.67 means the fund’s return this year is 1.67 standard deviations above its average, indicating an unusually strong performance for the year, higher than 95% of previous returns.
